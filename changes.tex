This file records proofreading findings that are larger than local wording,
punctuation, or notation fixes. I did not silently rewrite these parts in the
LyX source because each one affects a proof structure, a theorem convention, or
an extended example. For each item below, I describe the issue and a concrete
repair path.

\section{cutting\_plane\_volume.lyx: final set is not necessarily a cuboid}

\textbf{Location/context.} In the volume lower-bound argument for cutting along
coordinate directions, the text says that when the algorithm stops, the
remaining set ``must be an axis-parallel cuboid'' with side lengths in a certain
range, and then uses the product of these widths as the final volume lower
bound.

\textbf{Issue.} Intersecting an arbitrary convex body with a sequence of
axis-parallel halfspaces does not, by itself, make the surviving body an
axis-parallel cuboid. It only constrains the surviving body to lie inside a
box, and possibly to contain some smaller structured subset if an additional
invariant is proved. Therefore the product of final coordinate widths is not
automatically the volume of the remaining convex set, nor even automatically a
lower bound on its volume.

\textbf{Suggested repair.} Replace the claim by a statement of the exact
invariant available from the algorithm. Possible routes:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Prove that the current body always contains an axis-parallel cuboid whose
side lengths are bounded below by the claimed quantities. Then the volume lower
bound follows from the contained cuboid.
\item Weaken the conclusion to a width/box-containment statement and avoid
using the product of widths as a volume lower bound.
\item Restrict the argument to an adversarial body that is itself maintained as
an axis-parallel cuboid, if that is the intended lower-bound construction.
\end{enumerate}

\section{har\_dikin.lyx: inequality directions in the boundary-volume estimate}

\textbf{Location/context.} In the proof around the estimate
\[
(1-\gamma)\operatorname{vol}(sB^n)
 \leq \operatorname{vol}((x+sB^n\setminus K)
 \leq p\operatorname{vol}(sB^n)-\operatorname{vol}((s/2)B^n),
\]
the next displayed conclusion is \(p\leq 1-\gamma+2^{-n}\).

\textbf{Issue.} The displayed line has a missing parenthesis around
\((x+sB^n)\setminus K\), and the inequality directions do not appear to imply
the following bound on \(p\). The direction needed for the next line is of the
form
\[
(1-\gamma)\operatorname{vol}(sB^n)
 \geq \operatorname{vol}((x+sB^n)\setminus K)
 \geq p\operatorname{vol}(sB^n)-\operatorname{vol}((s/2)B^n).
\]
This is not just a typographical punctuation change: it is part of the proof's
measure comparison.

\textbf{Suggested repair.} Recheck the geometric containment that bounds
\(\operatorname{vol}((x+sB^n)\setminus K)\). If the intended implication is
\(p\leq 1-\gamma+2^{-n}\), revise the displayed inequality to the direction
above and add one sentence explaining why the excluded \((s/2)B^n\) term is the
only loss.

\section{interior\_point\_method.lyx: transformed LP lower bound}

\textbf{Location/context.} In the reduction from a general linear program to a
bounded transformed problem, the proof uses a display of the form
\[
\cdots \leq \frac{\delta}{LR}\,\overline{\mathrm{OPT}}+\delta^2
\]
and later lower-bounds the transformed objective using
\(\|\frac{1}{R}x\|_1\) for a transformed feasible point.

\textbf{Issue.} The preceding display appears to compare
\(\overline{\mathrm{OPT}}\) to \(\mathrm{OPT}\), not to itself. In addition, the
later lower bound treats a transformed feasible vector as though it directly
came from an original feasible point. That loses the distinction between
\(\overline{x}_{1:n}\) and an original feasible \(x\).

\textbf{Suggested repair.} Use the original optimum in the comparison, e.g.
\[
\cdots \leq \frac{\delta}{LR}\,\mathrm{OPT}+\delta^2,
\]
and derive the lower bound directly from the transformed constraints. One
candidate route is to use a bound such as
\(\|\overline{x}_{1:n}\|_1\leq n+1\), which gives a lower bound of the form
\[
\frac{\delta}{L}c^\top\overline{x}_{1:n}\geq -\delta(n+1)
\]
under the normalization used in the proof. The exact constant should be checked
against the surrounding scaling.

\section{subspace\_embedding.lyx: net proof states only a one-sided bound}

\textbf{Location/context.} In the proof of a subspace embedding from a finite
net, the source uses incomplete notation such as \(N=\{x_i\}_{i=1}\) and the
final probability statement bounds only
\[
\mathbb{P}\!\left(x^\top(U^\top\Pi^\top\Pi U-I)x\leq 4\epsilon\right).
\]

\textbf{Issue.} A subspace embedding requires a uniform two-sided/operator-norm
bound. A one-sided quadratic-form statement for an unspecified \(x\) does not
imply the desired OSE guarantee. The finite net should also be denoted as a set
with a cardinality, e.g. \(\mathcal{N}=\{x_i\}_{i=1}^N\), and the proof should
carry absolute values through the quadratic form.

\textbf{Suggested repair.} State the net argument as: for every unit vector
\(x\), compare \(x\) to a nearby \(y\in\mathcal N\), control
\[
\left|x^\top(U^\top\Pi^\top\Pi U-I)x\right|,
\]
and conclude
\[
\mathbb{P}\!\left(
\|U^\top\Pi^\top\Pi U-I_d\|_{\mathrm{op}}\leq 4\epsilon
\right)\geq 1-5^d\delta
\]
or the corresponding constant used in the chapter.

\section{appendix.lyx: Holder proof sign and absolute values}

\textbf{Location/context.} The appendix proof of Holder's inequality sets up a
KKT proof using an objective like \(f(x)=-\sum_i x_i y_i\), stationarity, and
powers such as \(x_i^p\).

\textbf{Issue.} The current proof appears to have a sign mismatch:
if \(f(x)=-\sum_i x_i y_i\), then \(\nabla f=-y\), not \(y\). The proof also
uses powers of coordinates without explicitly reducing to nonnegative vectors
or using absolute-value derivatives. For general real vectors, the derivatives
should involve \(|x_i|^{p-2}x_i\), or the proof should first reduce to
nonnegative \(x_i,y_i\).

\textbf{Suggested repair.} Either:
\begin{enumerate}
\item state at the beginning that it suffices to prove the inequality for
nonnegative vectors and then use \(x_i^p\), or
\item keep general real vectors and write the stationarity condition with
\(|x_i|^{p-2}x_i\).
\end{enumerate}
Also fix the sign convention in the Lagrangian/stationarity equations so that
it is consistent with the chosen objective.

\section{equivalence.lyx: automatic differentiation chain-rule notation}

\textbf{Location/context.} In the automatic differentiation discussion, the
source introduces \(L_i\) as a set of variables, later uses it as a set of
indices, and then writes terms like \(x_{j_{-1}}\), \(x_{j_{-2}}\), etc.

\textbf{Issue.} I corrected the local definition of \(L_i\) to be a set of
indices, but the negative-index notation remains confusing and is not standard.
It risks obscuring the actual chain-rule statement.

\textbf{Suggested repair.} Replace the negative-index explanatory formula by a
plain statement: after fixing all variables except \(x_i\) and the children
\(x_j\) with \(j\in L_i\), each \(x_j\) is a function of \(x_i\), and hence
\[
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}
 =\sum_{j\in L_i}\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}
  \frac{\partial x_j}{\partial x_i}.
\]
This keeps the chain rule clear without inventing notation for ``all other
children.''

\section{langevin\_dynamics.lyx: log-Sobolev constant convention}

\textbf{Location/context.} The chapter states that for a logconcave measure with
support diameter \(D\), the log-Sobolev constant is \(\Omega(1/D)\).

\textbf{Issue.} The chapter defines \(C_{LSI}\) in the convention
\[
d_{KL}(\rho,\nu)\leq \frac{C_{LSI}}{2}J_\nu(\rho).
\]
Under this convention, results sometimes quoted as \(\Omega(1/D)\) may be using
the reciprocal convention. The statement should specify the convention and any
extra assumptions, such as isotropic normalization, required by the cited
result.

\textbf{Suggested repair.} Recheck the cited theorem's convention. If the cited
constant is reciprocal to \(C_{LSI}\), rewrite the sentence as a bound on
\(1/C_{LSI}\), or state the equivalent \(C_{LSI}=O(D)\) (or the correct power of
\(D\)) under the assumptions used in the theorem.

\section{mirror\_descent.lyx: unit-ball example starts outside the ball}

\textbf{Location/context.} In the unit-ball example, the text starts from a
point of the form
\[
x=(1,1/n,1/n,\ldots,1/n)
\]
and later derives a step-size restriction \(h\leq 1/n\).

\textbf{Issue.} The displayed starting point is outside the Euclidean unit ball:
its first coordinate already has magnitude \(1\), and the remaining coordinates
add positive squared norm. Thus the example does not satisfy the stated domain.

\textbf{Suggested repair.} Use a scaled point such as
\[
x=(1/2,1/(2n),\ldots,1/(2n))
\]
or another point strictly inside the unit ball, and adjust the later step-size
bound accordingly, e.g. \(h\leq 1/(2n)\) if the same scaling is used.
